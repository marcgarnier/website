PhD Researcher · AI & politics · Université Laval

Tracing how Canada frames artificial intelligence.

I am a doctoral researcher in political science at Université Laval, working under the supervision of Thierry Giasson. My research uses computational text analysis to map how artificial intelligence has been framed in Canadian political documents — from the 2017 Pan-Canadian AI Strategy to the national strategies of 2026.

About

Political science, with a computational toolbox.

Trained in sociology (Aix-Marseille University) and political science (Sciences Po Aix), I now study the political construction of AI in Canada. Before the doctorate, I worked in communications, public relations and research assistantships on both sides of the Atlantic — experience that shapes how I think about political messaging, media framing and public institutions.

Axis 01 · Frames

Which frames?

The thematic dimensions mobilised around AI — economy, ethics, security, sovereignty — across eight years of federal discourse.

Axis 02 · Messengers

Which messengers?

The actors who carry and legitimise each framing in the public arena: researchers, civil servants, firms, civil society, parties.

Axis 03 · Trajectory

Which trajectory?

How dominant framings shift over time — measured quantitatively from 2017 to 2026 with supervised classification.
